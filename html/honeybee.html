About HoneyBees

Honey bees (genus Apis) are highly social insects renowned for living in large, structured colonies, constructing hexagonal wax combs, and producing honey from nectar. A hive consists of one queen, hundreds of male drones, and thousands of female worker bees. They are crucial pollinators and are threatened by colony collapse disorder.

Key Facts about HoneyBees

1. Colony Structure: A single queen lays up to 2,500 eggs/day, living up to 5 years. Female workers (live ~6 weeks in summer) do all foraging and hive maintenance. Male drones (exist for mating) die after mating or are expelled before winter. 2. "Waggle Dance": Worker bees communicate the direction and distance of food sources (nectar and pollen) to other bees by performing a specialized "waggle dance" in the hive. 3. Diet & Production: Honey bees consume nectar and pollen. Nectar is regurgitated, digested, and stored as honey to survive winter. 4. Reproduction: Eggs hatch in 3 days, turning into larvae. Queens take 16 days to mature, workers ~21 days, and drones 24 days.
